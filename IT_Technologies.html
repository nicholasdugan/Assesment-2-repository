Autonomous Vehicles


What technological or other developments make this possible?

Autonomous vehicles are around now, the first autonomous vehicle was created by Nissan in 2013 and the first registered AV was created by Hyundai in 2016. Cruise control is a form of autonomous vehicle. In the next 3 years, more vehicles will be ‘more’ automated and have little to no human input. Autonomous vehicles, also known as self-driving cars, robo cars, driverless cars, etc. are cars that have none, or limited, human input. AVs rely on artificial intelligence (AI), sensors and big data to analyze information, adapt to changing circumstances and handle complex situations as a substitute for human judgement, as the latter would no longer be needed for conventional vehicle operations such as lane-changing, parking, collision avoidance and braking (Long, Hanford, Janrathitikarn, Sinsley, & Miller, 2007; West, 2016). Autonomous vehicles are in demand and have been for a long time. Since late 2009, early 2010, companies like Google and Waymo have been experimenting with autonomous vehicles. They were the first companies to introduce ‘front-crash prevention systems’ that use sensors that warn drivers of an impending obstacle and apply the brakes if the driver does not react fast enough. Waymo also uses high-resolution cameras and lidar (light detection and ranging) (How Google's Self-Driving Car Will Change Everything, 2021). Lidar uses laser beams to detect objects. Lidar SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) in 3D and Outdoor Visual Navigation (cameras) use algorithms to negotiate other vehicles and the public, this is combined by AI software called Navigation-technology Sensor fusion (Gardens by the Bay 2015).

Using SLAM, a vehicle can create a map as it travels down the road and detect obstacles in real time. It allows the vehicle to calculate where it is and how it is moving (mathworks 2021).

Outdoor Navigation systems utilize dual cameras for 3D imagery, the cameras are set apart and use triangulation for the detection of the position of objects as well as their speed. It can also use Time of Flight( ToF) cameras to measure the speed of light to make decisions. It records information in a single shot – creating the information rapidly in a pixel format. These 3D cameras perform poorly in low light (Future Markets Magazine). Other positioning and object detection technologies are also used depending on the weather, speed and density of objects including Radio Frequency Identification (RFID), radar, sonar and GPS. Modified traffic signs and signaling are also used. It is the Sensor fusion software that creates a smooth traversal and decides whether the Lidar laser system or the Outdoor Visual Navigation system is the system on which to use as input information. The development of these algorithms and advances in camera lenses has increased the preciseness that vehicles can operate in. Artificial intelligence is also being used to enable vehicles to identify and deal with adverse weather conditions such as torrential rain, there is currently limitations being able to operate in rain conditions past 30mm /h and deterioration in operation past 10mm /h (Land Transport Guru). It also will be used so that the vehicle can identify a hailing passenger (Ng, 2020). The costs of all this technology will limit it’s use in the near future for private vehicles. But for public transport namely buses, there are already commercial vehicles in production such as Volvo’s 7900 electric bus. Trials and some fledgling services are starting to happen around the world. Trial autonomous bus services have been launched in Singapore in two industrial parks, which are the first in the country to be a variable price on demand service, there are also street sweepers operating at night in the west of the country (Jakarta Post 2021).

What is likely to change? Which people will be most affected and how?

With driverless cars, many people will be out of jobs. Bus drivers, ride share drivers, taxi drivers, food delivery drivers and many more job sectors will be impacted with the introduction of driverless cars. Another impact would be the decline of privately owned vehicles. This could result in less pollution and also make driverless cars cheaper. It would also make transportation for disabled and elderly people easier as human input is negated. Another way that emissions would be lowered is that self-driving cars will be practicing more eco-friendly driving patterns. If they practice this efficient driving behavior, they could reduce “unnecessary emissions up to 20%” (How Google's Self-Driving Car Will Change Everything, 2021).
High density cities such as Singapore envisage autonomous vehicles providing last mile public transport reducing the need for private cars and freeing up land resources dedicated to cars for other uses (LTA Singapore 2020).
Insurance is likely to be cheaper as “driver error” accidents are less likely to occur. Hospital trauma centers and recuperative therapy businesses will no longer have business from injuries resulted from traffic accidents. As cars become more automated, they will be less likely to break the law (I am sure car enthusiasts will create work arounds!), this will reduce the need for traffic police and compliance devices such as speed/red light cameras and speed humps. New types of intersections will emerge as autonomous vehicles in the future will be able to collaborate on the most effective way to move around each other, not like current intersections which as designed on rules people on the most part can negotiate.
Support services will be created to keep cars compliant to the latest software and technology levels for safe interaction with roadside systems and other cars. Security systems will also need to be developed to now not only keep out physical thieves but malicious software.

Cyber Security

Cybersecurity is the increasingly important subject of safeguarding computer data against malicious or outsider threats. Everybody is affected by cybersecurity, as nearly all governments, organizations and individuals rely on IT systems and the data it contains. By implementing policies and protocols within IT networks and educating employees on common methods of cyber-attack, cybersecurity professionals work to safeguard their organizations data. Cybersecurity covers a broad area of techniques and counter measures to prevent malicious users or organizations from penetrating their systems. Common cybersecurity techniques include Network Security, Data Loss Prevention, Intrusion Detection Systems/Intrusion Prevention Systems and anti-virus/anti-malware software (Forcepoint, 2021). One of the most cutting edge and emerging security techniques is called “Deception Technology”, which creates lures and traps in data that looks legitimate to deceive an intruder into accessing that data, which can generate an alert or warning for the cybersecurity professional or administrator(s) of that network. The data that the intruder is trying to access is commonly imitating legitimate data, hence “deceiving” the intruder. Originally the idea of this came from the “Honeypot” technique, which also mimics a legitimate computer system that likely has valuable information (Symanovich, 2020), and has since morphed into deceptive technology by using multiple honeypots all throughout the network.


This technique can provide real-time threat reporting, rather than the intruder gaining access and responders only finding out after significant damage is done, which can cost organizations millions of dollars in lost data and productivity. In the Mandiant Security Effectiveness Report 2020, it was found that 53% of cyber-attacks in businesses included in the report were missed, and only 33% were prevented by already implemented security controls (FireEye Incorporated, 2020). Introducing deceptive technology may eventually be the most common way to detect intruders making their way into different networks. Rather than working on keeping malicious threats out of systems, corporations and government may need to instead work on encrypting all of their data, and make it impossible or highly unlikely for cybercriminals to decipher this data by keeping the encryption keys distributed(Lemos, n.d.). Encrypting a whole organizations data may be a costly and time inefficient strategy, so potentially this may only be open to businesses and governments that can afford that kind of technology. The future of cybersecurity may be in the hands of Artificial Intelligence according to Cybersecurity Researcher Attila Tomaschek. “The future of cybersecurity will have a heavy focus on using artificial intelligence (AI) to secure devices and systems in the increasingly connected world”, says Tomaschek in an article with Disruptor Daily. Using “Human oversight” is drastically ineffective and resource consuming, whereas having an AI system that can analyze and neutralize these threats automatically can help take the workload off human resources. AI can be utilized to consume massive amount of data from all different types of sources, with machine learning the AI can be fully aware of all the different types of risks and threats out on the web. Legacy cybersecurity technologies are unable to keep up with the immense number of threats that organizations and governments face every day, whereas AI systems can provide cybersecurity professionals with the analytics and information to keep them informed to make the right decisions during cyber breaches. The very nature of cybersecurity is one of constant change and of constant catch-up with cyber threats, so technologies like AI and deception will be of ongoing use in the future.

The impact of using Artificial Intelligence within cybersecurity will see the automation of highly repetitive tasks that are likely done by cybersecurity professionals already, freeing up their time for other critical tasks. AI can also use its knowledge of previous intrusions and cyber-attacks to scan through data and detect patterns to then allow cybersecurity professionals to make informed decisions about the current threats that could plague their organization. Because of this technology, cybersecurity teams would likely have more time and resources to use on more demanding tasks and with 51 percent of organizations reporting to a have a shortage of cyber security skills in 2017 and 70 percent of Information Systems Security Association members believing that their organization had been impacted by a global shortage in cybersecurity skills (Oltsik J, 2020). This massive understaffing and shortage of cybersecurity skill has led to high burnout rates and puts more pressure on current staff to perform. The understaffing of the cyber industry is cause for alarm, as the professionals that consumers, businesses and governments put their trust in to safeguard our data against malicious threats are being overworked and are burning out, so more emphasis needs to be placed on filling these roles, from primary school to university. In Australia, coding classes have become mandatory in Queensland as the government looks to fill the IT skills gap within many industries and this will likely lead on to the development of more cybersecurity professionals. Using AI within cybersecurity will likely allow staff to use their time efficiently and more effectively respond to cyber threats.

Robots

A robot is a machine that is controlled internally or a machine that is designed to accomplish a task and uses programs to make decisions. There are a large range of different types of robots.

Nano robots are to be used in medicine and are currently being tested on animals. These can carry medicines to specific places in the body for things such as cancer treatment. Testing at this moment, has mice being treated for brain tumours. In the future it is envisioned that nano or micro robots could be injected into the blood stream and carry out maintenance such as plaque removal.

New to medicine are miniature robots called Vivo robots, which are inserted by a small incision into the torso cavity and can be controlled remotely by experienced surgeons. These robots are inexpensive and are transportable and so can be used by less trained staff in remote locations. They also have the advantage of providing scarless surgery.

Small robots are being used for colonoscopy procedures, these imitate worms in their movement and greatly reduce the surgical risk of the procedure for example: causing a perforation of the bowel. As these devices can move themselves, they do not need to be pushed into position.

Larger robots are being used for camera positions and holding instruments in place. Currently the restrictions on more robotics in medicine is limited by the lack of dexterity of a robot and the lack of a sense of touch (Thomas 2021).

Swarm robots or UAV (unmanned aerial vehicles) drones are currently in use, mainly for advertising or public displays such as New Year’s Eve celebrations. The number of robots in these displays and their complexity is constantly increasing. Recently Korean car manufacturer Genesis flew 3,218 drones in Shanghai to mark their entry into the Chinese market.

Blockchain Technology and Cryptocurrency

What does it do?
A cryptocurrency is a type of digital currency. Bitcoin is the most popular one. It does not physically exist; it only has a digital presence. They are heavily encrypted; they differ to credit cards as they are not tied to financial institutions – it is a peer-to-peer transaction between two parties. You carry your digital currency in a digital wallet. It is cheaper and faster than credit cards as it takes minutes to clear, not a few days (techtarget).

A digital wallet can be used for financial transactions other than a cryptocurrency. Digital wallets can also be used to store credit cards for transaction payment methods such as PayPal or Apple Pay. These work by replacing a credit card number with a tokenized Device Primary Account Number. These still have the back-end credit card processing delay (corporate finance institute).
Blockchain is an open ledger in the public domain that can be viewed by anyone. All transactions are their displaying what digital wallets transferred digital currency to which digital currency.
The identity of the digital wallet is encrypted. Currently there is no legislation or regulations around it. Governments are hence promoting digital wallets using digital players such as Apple while they grapple with regulatory control while admitting to the security advantages of digital wallets.

Blockchain is a type of database used by some cryptocurrencies to create a ledger. Blockchains store data in blocks and chain them together – hence the imaginative name. It can be used in other circumstances as a database other than just for cryptocurrencies. The data stored is irreversible – hence the ledger of transactions is permanent. Hence currencies cannot be reused making it secure against fraud. A block is a chunk of storage into which data is stored it has a finite space. When filled the block becomes non writable and it added to the chain with a timestamp. This then documents when transactions occurred. Blockchain can operate in a non-centralised manner, what this means is that multiple copies of the database are stored on servers or clusters of servers called ‘node’. Each node has a copy of every transaction that ever occurred in the blockchain. Nodes verify the legitimacy of their database with each other – in order to modify the database, the majority of nodes must agree. To be able to verify each other the ledger is transparent. Which is why the ledger can be publicly viewed, the wallet details are encrypted not the transaction. The blocks order is secured with a timestamp and a hash along with the hash of the block before it. To edit the block means the hash would change and hence the “chain” would be broken, and that node would become untrusted (Conway, L. 2020).

Cryptocurrencies have advantages by being extremely secure, but this has drawbacks as millions have been lost when passwords to digital wallets have been lost. It is believed that 20% of Bitcoin is idle (not engaged in transactions) and is probably due to lost passwords or the death of its owners. Its secure and secretive nature has appealed to criminals in transferring funds discreetly, as Bitcoin does not need to know the identity of the owner unlike having a bank account. Being peer to peer means that people do not have the resources like banks to secure their encryption keys and the digital wallets have become the target of thieves for these keys. Banks store their keys in Hardware Security Modules (HSM) a device that has no connection to the digital world. These are made by high grade security firms such as Thales (Thales).Another issue with cryptocurrencies is the dependency on data mining. This is energy intensive and hence not good for the environment or for the local infrastructure. It also will limit on how many transactions per second can be obtained. This was famously outlined in what happened in Iceland with the runaway success of their datacentres (Noack, Rick. 2018).
